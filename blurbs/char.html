They got a good characteristic..

Venkat Trivikram

Historically classification has been the most standard or natural way to study newer structures and spaces. To know more about a structure one would consider maps to it and from it. Owing to that, we see the following.

Let, \(\mathbb{F}\) be a field. Since, \[ 1_{\mathbb{F}} \in \mathbb{F},\; 1_{\mathbb{F}}+1_{\mathbb{F}} \in \mathbb{F}\cdots \] we can say, \(n\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} \in \mathbb{F}\) \(\forall\, n \in \mathbb{N}.\) Suppose, we define \(\forall\,n \in \mathbb{N}\),

\[ (-n)\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} := -(n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}), \]

then we can consider a map

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi : \mathbb{Z} &\rightarrow \mathbb{F}\\ n &\mapsto n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}. \end{aligned} \]

Observe, for any \(m,n \in \mathbb{Z}\)

  • \(\varphi(m + n) = (m+n)\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} = m\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} + n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}=\varphi(m)+\varphi(n)\)
  • \(\varphi(mn)=mn \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}=(m \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}})(n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}})=\varphi(m)\varphi(n)\)
  • \(\varphi(0)=0_{\mathbb{F}},\; \varphi(1)=1_{\mathbb{F}}\)

thus, \(\varphi\) is a ring homomorphism.

When we have a homomorphism, then the most immediate natural thing to do is to see its kernel. So,

\[ \ker(\varphi) = \{n \in \mathbb{Z} \mid n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} = 0_{\mathbb{F}}\}. \]

Now, for any non-zero integer \(n\) consider a decomposition \(n=ab\), then

\[ \begin{aligned} n\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} &= 0_{\mathbb{F}}\\ (ab)\cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} &= 0_{\mathbb{F}}\\ (a \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}})(b \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}) &= 0_{\mathbb{F}} \end{aligned} \]

Field being an integral domain, tells us, either \((a \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}) = 0_{\mathbb{F}}\) or \((b \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}})=0_{\mathbb{F}}.\) Now, one can further decompose \(a\) and \(b\) (if possible) and can find a smallest integer \(n\) such that \(n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}} = 0_{\mathbb{F}}\), this leads to a following definition.

Definition (Characteristic): In a field \(\mathbb{F}\), the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}=0_{\mathbb{F}}\) is called characteristic of a field, \(\cha(\mathbb{F})\).

If we are defining some observation, it better be important. But why this characteristic of a field important? How exactly is it characterizing the field. Let us delve into that.

since, a prime can't be decomposed further, the characteristic should be a prime if not zero.

Now, coming back to the \(\ker(\varphi)\). Since, \(\ker(\varphi)\) is an ideal of \(\mathbb{Z}\) and \(\mathbb{Z}\) is a PID, we say \(\ker(\varphi) = (d)\) for some \(d \in \mathbb{Z}\). Since, \(\cha(\mathbb{F})\) is the smallest \(n\) such that \(n \cdot 1_{\mathbb{F}}=0_{\mathbb{F}}\), \(\cha(\mathbb{F}) \in \ker(\varphi)\) and it thus must be a generator of \(\ker(\varphi)\). Thus, \(\ker(\varphi) \equiv \cha(\mathbb{F})\,\mathbb{Z}.\)

When we have the kernel, then the most immediate natural thing to do is to kill it, ahem, quotient it. So, look at

\[ \mathbb{Z}/ \cha(\mathbb{F})\,\mathbb{Z}. \]

We then, by First Isomorphism Theorem, have the following commutative diagram

where, \(\pi\) is the canonical surjection and \(\iota\) is injection. So, there is an isomorphic copy of \(\mathbb{Z}/ \text{ch}(\mathbb{F})\) in \(\mathbb{F}\). Since, \(\cha(\mathbb{F})=0 \text{ or } p\) for a prime \(p\). There are only two cases. We observe,

  • if \(\cha(\mathbb{F})=0\), then \(\mathbb{Z}/ \text{ch}(\mathbb{F})\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}\). Thus, there is an isomorphic copy of \(\mathbb{Z}\) in \(\mathbb{F}.\) But, \(\mathbb{F}\) is a field, thus field of fractions of \(\mathbb{Z}\) also sits in \(\mathbb{F}\) isomorphically. We know,

\[ \text{Frac}(\mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Q}. \]

Thus,

\[ \text{‚Äúan isomorphic copy of } \mathbb{Q} \text{ sits inside } \mathbb{F}\,\text{"}. \]

  • if \(\cha(\mathbb{F})=p\), for some prime \(p\), then there is an isomorphic copy of \(\mathbb{Z}/ p\mathbb{Z}\) in \(\mathbb{F}.\) But, \(\mathbb{Z}/ p\mathbb{Z}\) is already a field, we thus conclude,

\[ \text{‚Äúan isomorphic copy of } \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \text{ sits inside } \mathbb{F}\,\text{"}. \]

Thus, there essentially are two kinds of fields! Great right!. So, once the characteristic of the field is known, we can immediately say something about the field. So, characteristic really does characterize the field :)
