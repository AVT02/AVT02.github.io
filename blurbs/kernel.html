OK wait! let us arrive there and naturally complete the sentence.

We first recall, if \( G \) and \( H \) are groups and \( \varphi : G \rightarrow H \) is a group homomorphism, then we know that

\[ \ker(\varphi) := \{ g \in G \mid \varphi(g) = \text{id}_{H} \}, \] viz. \[ \ker(\varphi) = \varphi^{-1}(\text{id}_{H}). \]

This immediately reminds us of the following object

\[ \text{‚Äúfiber".}\]

We recall, \( A, B \) are sets and \( f : A \rightarrow B \) be a function, then for each \( b \in B, \) the preimage of \( \{b\} \) under \( f \) is called

\[ \text{fiber of } f \text{ over } b. \]

We realize, \( \ker(\varphi) \) is a fiber of \( \varphi \) over \( \text{id}_H. \) Nice, now since there are many fibers of \( \varphi \), it is tempting to conclude that kernel is the important fiber. But, what makes it important among the fibers?

To see that, for any \( g \in G \), look at the coset

\[ g \ker(\varphi) := \{ gk \in G \mid k \in \ker(\varphi) \}. \]

This set, under \( \varphi \) maps to...

\[ \varphi(gk) = \varphi(g)\varphi(k) = \varphi(g)\,\text{id}_H = \varphi(g) \]

So, \( g\ker(\varphi) \subseteq \varphi^{-1}(\varphi(g)). \) Suppose, \( h \in \varphi^{-1}(\varphi(g)). \) Then, \( \varphi(h) = \varphi(g). \) Thus,

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi(h)\varphi(g)^{-1} &= \text{id}_H \\ \varphi(h)\varphi(g^{-1}) &= \text{id}_H \\ \varphi(hg^{-1}) &= \text{id}_H \\ hg^{-1} &\in \ker(\varphi) \\ h &\in g\ker(\varphi). \end{aligned} \]

Thus, \( \varphi^{-1}(\varphi(g)) \subseteq g\ker(\varphi). \) Thus, \( \varphi^{-1}(\varphi(g)) \equiv g\ker(\varphi) \), viz.

\[ g\ker(\varphi) \text{ is precisely the fiber over } \varphi(g). \]

We thus have following bijective correspondence

\[ \{g \ker(\varphi)\} \overset{\varphi}{\longleftrightarrow} \{\varphi(g)\}. \]

Since, fibers are disjoint by definition, each element in the image of \( \varphi \) is uniquely determined by the corresponding coset consisting \( \ker(\varphi). \) This forms the basic idea of the celebrated First Isomorphism Theorem which states:

if \( G \) and \( H \) are groups and \( \varphi : G \rightarrow H \) is a group homomorphism, then \[ G / \ker(\varphi) \cong \operatorname{Im}(\varphi). \]
