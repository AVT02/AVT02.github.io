Into the Product

Venkat Trivikram

Roughly speaking, a topology is nothing but a good subset of the power set right? So, we can talk about set inclusions of topologies. We recall that coarsest topology on a set \(X\) is the smallest topology in the sense of inclusions of all topologies on the set \(X\). So, any open set in the coarsest topology is also open in any other topology on that set. Let us keep this aside for time being.

When do we call some set, a space? It is when that set is endowed with some topology. Consider a topological space \(X\) with topology \(\tau\). Consider a subset \(S\) of \(X\). When do we call this subset a subspace? As mentioned above, the answer is that it is when that subset is endowed with a topology. So we have to define open sets on \(S\). But we already have a topology in hand to start with, which is the topology on the whole set, \(\tau\).

So, natural way to define open sets on \(S\) is: you look at all the open sets \(X\), i.e., the elements of \(\tau\), and declare those to be open in \(S\) which intersect \(S\). Nice, well and good. But, there is another way to look at the same thing.

We recall definition of continuous function. A function between two topological spaces \(f : X \to Y\) is continuous if for every open set in \(Y\), the inverse image w.r.t \(f\) is open in \(X\). This should ring a bell that, a way to look at open sets on a set is to consider a map to some other known1 space and consider the inverse image of the open sets in that space. Good, no?

So, that's the idea, we now make it rigorous. We have subset \(S\) of \(X\). What is the most natural map from \(S\) to a known space? Firstly, here the known space is \(X\). So, the answer is the inclusion map

\[ \iota : S \hookrightarrow X \]

since, \(S\) is literally the subset of \(X\).

Consider the collection of all inverse images of open sets of \(X\), i.e.

\[ \mathcal{O} := \{\iota^{-1}(U) \mid U \in \tau\}. \]

Now, look at all topologies of \(S\). Moreover, look at those topologies in which \(\mathcal{O}\) is a subset of. So, as our instinct says, we have to declare one of these topologies as ‚Äúthe‚Äù topology on \(S\). For sanity reasons, we don't need many open sets, right? we are content with the bare minimum. Thus, we look at the coarsest topology among all the topologies on \(S\) that contain \(\mathcal{O}\).

So, we are looking at the coarsest topology on \(S\) such that inclusion \(\iota : S \hookrightarrow X\) is continuous. The beauty of this is that it is equivalent2 to the notion that I first mentioned. Thus, this is the subspace topology.

Now we come to the real deal. Let \(I\) be any index set. For topological spaces \(X_\alpha, \alpha \in I\), we can make sense of cartesian product

\[ P := \prod_{\alpha \in I} X_\alpha. \]

What would be a good way to define topology on \(P\)? We follow the second aforementioned idea of continuous functions. So, we must have continuous functions from \(P\) to some known space(s). What other well-known spaces do we have in comparison to the components of the product, namely \(X_\alpha, \alpha \in I\). And what more are the natural functions from the product to the component spaces than the projections? Thus, we look at the coarsest topology on \(P\) such that all projections

\[ \pi_\alpha : P \to X_\alpha \]

are continuous. We call this the product topology on \(P\).
